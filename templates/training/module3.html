Module 3

The Sandbox

You will learn how to use your personal practice space on Wikipedia, where nothing you do affects the real encyclopedia. By the end, you will have formatted text, created links, written a citation, and saved real edits, not just read about them.
Section 1 of 7

What is a sandbox and why does it matter?

Every Wikipedia editor gets a sandbox. It is a page that belongs to you, lives on Wikipedia's servers, and works exactly like a real article, except nothing you do there shows up anywhere public. You can write, delete, experiment, break things, start over. Nobody cares. That is the whole point.

Your sandbox address
User:YourUsername/sandbox

Click your username in the top right corner of any Wikipedia page and select "Sandbox" to get there. Keep it open in another tab for the rest of this module. You are going to use it a lot.

Why not just practice on real articles? Because the learning curve is real, and the worst way to learn is by breaking something on a page that thousands of people are reading. The sandbox lets you figure things out at your own speed with zero consequences. Every confident editor you see on Wikipedia started right here.

Section 2 of 7

Choosing your editing tool

Wikipedia has two editing tools. Pick the right one now and save yourself some confusion.

Start here
VisualEditor
Works like Google Docs. Click, type, use toolbar buttons. What you see is roughly what readers will see. Best for learning.
Later
Source Editor
Shows raw code with markup like == Heading == and [[links]]. More control, steeper curve. Not needed yet.

To switch between them, look for the pencil icon at the top of any editable page. If you see a bunch of brackets and equal signs, you are in Source Editor. Switch back to VisualEditor.

One more thing: use a laptop or desktop for this module. You can make quick edits on your phone, but for learning the tools, a bigger screen makes a real difference.

Section 3 of 7

Formatting basics

Open your sandbox in another tab. You are going to follow along.

Bold and italics. Highlight text, click B or I in the toolbar. Bold the first time the subject's name appears in an article. Italics for book titles, film titles, albums, and foreign-language words.

Headings. Use the paragraph dropdown menu (says "Paragraph" by default) to select Heading, Sub-heading 1, etc. Headings create the table of contents on longer articles and make the page scannable.

Links. Highlight a word, click the chain-link icon, type the name of the Wikipedia article you want to link to. Wikipedia suggests matches as you type. If the article exists, you will see it in the dropdown. Internal links are how Wikipedia connects topics together.

Lists. Toolbar buttons for bulleted or numbered lists. Bullets for unordered items, numbers for sequences.

That covers what you will use in 90% of your editing. Tables, templates, and infoboxes exist but come later.

Section 4 of 7

Writing citations

This is the most important skill in the entire training. All the formatting and linking in the world does not matter if your content is not cited. Uncited claims get removed. Well-cited content survives almost anything.

A citation connects a statement to a published source. Those little numbered brackets [1] [2] [3] you see in Wikipedia articles? Each one links to a source at the bottom of the page.

Adding a citation in VisualEditor: Place your cursor at the end of the sentence. Click "Cite" in the toolbar. Three options: Automatic (paste a URL, Wikipedia tries to build the citation for you, always double-check the result), Manual (fill in author, title, publisher, date yourself), or Re-use (reference a source you already cited elsewhere in the article). Click "Insert" and you will see a numbered bracket appear.

Before you cite anything, though, you need to know what counts as a reliable source.

Section 5 of 7

Edit summaries and good habits

Every time you save an edit, Wikipedia asks you to describe what you changed. This is the edit summary. It is easy to skip. Never skip it.

Edit summaries are how other editors know what you did without reading every change. A clear summary builds trust. A blank one draws suspicion, especially on sensitive articles.

Good summaries
"Added citation from AP News for 2024 policy change"
"Fixed broken link in references"
"Expanded career section, two new sources"
Bad summaries
"Updated"
"Fixed stuff"
(blank)

Minor edit checkbox: Only use it for small, uncontroversial changes like fixing a typo or correcting a broken link. Never check it when you add content, change facts, or add citations. Marking a real edit as "minor" hides your work from people who filter those out, and it looks like you are trying to avoid attention.

Preview before you save. Before clicking "Publish changes," review your work. Catches formatting mistakes, broken links, and citation issues before they go live.

Section 6 of 7

Building your watchlist

Wikipedia has a built-in way to track articles you care about. Your watchlist shows you every change made to any article you are watching. To add an article, go to the page and click the star icon next to the title. The star fills in and that article is on your list.

Why this matters: If you edit an article and someone removes your citation two days later, your watchlist shows you that change. You can review it and respond, whether that means reverting or starting a Talk Page discussion. Without a watchlist, you would never know.

Your watchlist also teaches you to read diffs. A diff shows what changed between two versions of an article: added text in green, removed text in red. Scanning diffs quickly is a skill you will use constantly.

Practice
Set up your watchlist
Go to Special:Watchlist (the bell icon) and start watching articles you care about.
Found your watchlist page

Your watchlist is set. Check it every few days to see what is happening on articles you care about.

What comes next

You now have the core mechanical skills: formatting, links, citations, edit summaries, and a watchlist. These are the tools you will use in every edit from here on.

Module 4 is where you put all of this together on a live article. You will go deeper on article structure, learn more about finding and evaluating sources, and make your first substantive edit to a real Wikipedia page.

Before you move on, spend more time in your sandbox if you want. The more comfortable the tools feel, the more confident you will be when you are editing something thousands of people might read. No rush.
