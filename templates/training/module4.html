You will learn how articles are structured, go deeper on sourcing strategy, and make your first edit to a live Wikipedia article. By the end of this module, you will have changed something on Wikipedia that other people can read.
Section 1 of 6

How Wikipedia articles are structured

Before you edit an article, you need to understand what you are looking at. Wikipedia articles follow a consistent structure, and knowing the parts helps you figure out where your work fits.

The lead section comes first, before any headings. It summarizes the entire article in one to four paragraphs. On shorter articles the lead might be the whole thing. On longer articles it is an overview readers can scan before diving deeper.

Here is what matters about the lead: it gets the most traffic. Many readers never scroll past it. Improving the lead is high-impact work. But leads are watched closely, so your sourcing needs to be solid.

Section headings organize the rest. Biographical articles commonly use "Early life," "Career," "Personal life." Policy articles might use "Background," "Provisions," "Implementation," "Reception." These are conventions, not strict rules.

References live at the bottom. You built one of these in your sandbox already.

Categories and infoboxes are metadata. Categories sort the article into groups (like "Category:Puerto Rican activists"). Infoboxes are the summary tables in the top-right corner showing key facts at a glance.

A useful habit: Before editing any article, find a well-developed article on a similar topic. Study how it is organized. Use it as a structural model.

Section 2 of 6

Finding sources that hold up

You practiced citations in Module 3. Now the question is where to look. Not all reliable sources are equally useful, and knowing where to search saves time.

Current events and policy
AP News, Reuters, NYT, Washington Post, BBC, NPR. For Latino-specific coverage: Univision, Telemundo, El Pais, La Opinion, Latino USA. Local papers for local topics.
Health and science
Peer-reviewed journals via PubMed and Google Scholar. Government agencies: CDC, WHO, NIH. Be cautious with advocacy orgs as primary sources for health claims, even ones you agree with.
History and culture
Academic books and journal articles. Digitized newspaper archives for timelines. Oral history projects and university archives if published or reviewed in a form Wikipedia accepts.
Biographical articles
Published interviews, news profiles, institutional records. A person's own website works for basic facts (birth date, bibliography) but not evaluative claims.

Google Scholar is your best friend. Search there before regular Google. It surfaces academic sources that Wikipedia editors respect. Many papers are free, and abstracts often have enough for a citation.

Check the article's existing references too. The publications already cited may have additional coverage you can use. This is also how you spot weak sources that need replacing.

Section 3 of 6

Types of edits (and where to start)

Not all edits carry the same weight, and as a new editor, some are more strategic than others.

Best starting point
Adding citations to unsourced claims
Wikipedia is full of [citation needed] tags. Finding a reliable source and adding the citation is useful, low-conflict, and builds your credibility. It also protects the content from deletion.
Expanding thin sections
Many articles about underrepresented communities exist but are stubs. Adding a well-sourced paragraph to a thin section fills a real gap.
Correcting errors
Outdated statistics, wrong dates, broken links, factual mistakes. Straightforward. Always cite your correction.
Improving the lead
High impact but draws more attention. Make sure sources are airtight before touching a lead section.
Creating new content
Writing new sections or paragraphs. The most ambitious type. Build up to this after smaller edits.
What to avoid as a new editor: Do not start with heavily protected or contested articles (major political figures, active controversies, visible edit wars). These attract aggressive editors. Start with articles that have clear gaps and less traffic. Your edits will survive, you will build confidence, and your edit history earns credibility for bigger articles later.

Section 5 of 6

When your edit gets challenged

It will happen at some point. Not every time, maybe not on your first edit, but someone will revert your work or question your sources. This is normal.

If your edit is reverted: Do not redo the same edit. That starts an edit war. Go to the talk page. Cite your source, explain what gap you were filling, point to the policy that supports your approach. Keep the tone neutral even if you think the revert was wrong. Most of the time, a calm explanation resolves it.

If someone tags your content: Tags like [citation needed] or [dubious] are feedback, not attacks. Respond by adding a stronger source, clarifying the wording, or explaining on the talk page why the content is solid.

If you encounter persistent opposition: Some articles have editors who resist certain content. After attempting talk page discussion, you can escalate:

RfC
Request for comment
Opens the discussion to the broader Wikipedia community.
3O
Third opinion
A neutral editor reviews the dispute and gives their assessment.
WP
WikiProject support
Editors in WP:LATINOS or other projects can review and weigh in.
CD
ClanDi community
Your fellow editors on Discord for support and advice.

The key principle: your sources do the arguing for you. The better cited your content is, the harder it is for anyone to justify removing it.
