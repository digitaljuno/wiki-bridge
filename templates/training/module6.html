Section 1 of 5

Creating a new article

Everything so far has been improving existing content. Creating a new article is different. You are building from nothing, and Wikipedia has specific expectations.

The first question: is the subject notable? That means "significant coverage in reliable, independent sources." You need at least two or three published sources not connected to the subject itself. A person's own website or a press release from their employer does not count.

This is where systemic bias hits hardest. Many people and topics that matter to our communities have not received mainstream coverage. That does not mean they are not notable. It means you may need to dig harder: ethnic media, academic databases, local press archives. If the sources exist but are just harder to find, the subject is notable. If the sources genuinely do not exist, the article will likely get nominated for deletion.

How to create a new article
1
Draft in your sandbox or the Draft namespace. Write at User:YourUsername/sandbox or Draft:Article Name. Do not publish directly.
2
Structure it like a real article. Lead section, body sections by theme, citations throughout, categories at the bottom.
3
Get feedback. Share the draft with your WikiProject or on ClanDi Discord before publishing.
4
Submit or publish. From the Draft namespace, click "Submit for review." A volunteer reviewer evaluates it (can take days or weeks).
Common rejection reasons: Insufficient sources, promotional tone, reading like an essay instead of an encyclopedia entry, or not establishing notability. If rejected, read the feedback, fix what was flagged, resubmit.

Section 2 of 5

Translating articles between English and Spanish

This is one of the highest-impact things you can do as a bilingual editor. Millions of people read Spanish Wikipedia, and many topics with full English coverage either do not exist in Spanish or are barely there. Translation does not just duplicate information. It makes knowledge accessible to an entirely different audience.

Using the Content Translation tool
1
Enable it. Preferences > Beta features > check "Content Translation." Save.
2
Start a translation. Go to Special:ContentTranslation. Choose source and target languages, search for the article.
3
Translate with context. Split screen: original on the left, your translation on the right. Machine translation is a starting point. Rewrite for clarity. Adapt cultural references.
4
Adjust sources. Replace or supplement English sources with Spanish-language ones when possible.
5
Publish. It goes live on the target language Wikipedia.
Do not publish raw machine translations. Wikipedia actively patrols for this and will tag or delete articles that are clearly unreviewed machine output. The Content Translation tool is a starting point, not a finished product.
Practice

Section 3 of 5

Uploading images to Wikimedia Commons

Articles with images get more views and are taken more seriously. Many articles about underrepresented communities are missing images entirely. If you have a relevant photo, you can fix that.

Wikimedia Commons is the media library shared across all Wikimedia projects. Images you upload there can be used in any Wikipedia article in any language.

How to upload
1
Go to commons.wikimedia.org and log in (same credentials as Wikipedia).
2
Click "Upload" and select your file.
3
Fill in what the image shows, when and where it was taken, who took it.
4
Choose a license. CC-BY-SA 4.0 is recommended.
5
Add relevant categories so people can find the image.

What you can upload: Photos you took, original artwork you created, images clearly in the public domain (U.S. government works, expired copyrights).

What you cannot upload: Images you found online, screenshots from media, photos taken by someone else (unless they explicitly released them under a free license).

Always get consent from identifiable people. If your photo shows someone's face clearly enough to identify them, get their permission before uploading.

Where your images make the biggest difference: Event photos, community landmarks, cultural celebrations, images of public figures at public events. These are exactly the kinds of images missing from articles about Latino communities.

Section 4 of 5

Sustaining your practice

This is the end of the formal training. What happens next depends on you. Here is what we know about editors who stay active versus those who drop off.

Edit regularly, even in small amounts
One edit a week is better than a burst of twenty followed by silence. Your watchlist stays current, your editor relationships stay warm, your skills stay sharp.
Stay connected to a community
WP:LATINOS, Art+Feminism, ClanDi Discord, a few trusted collaborators. Having people who care about the same content areas makes the work sustainable.
Measure your impact
Your newcomer homepage and ClanDi dashboard track edits and page views. Seeing that a paragraph you wrote has been read 50,000 times keeps people going.
Teach someone else
The single most effective way to deepen your own skills. You now know enough to get someone started. ClanDi exists to make that easier.
Practice
Set your plan
Before you finish, commit to something specific.
Set a weekly editing goal (as small as one edit per week)
Identified one article to translate or one new article to create
Confirmed you are active in at least one community (WikiProject, Discord, or cohort)
You have a plan. That is what separates a training from a practice.
